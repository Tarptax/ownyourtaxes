Many musicians earn income from more than performances and merchandise sales. As artists create, record, publish, and distribute music, additional income opportunities often become available through royalties and licensing arrangements.

Understanding the basics of music-related income can help musicians better organize their records, monitor revenue streams, and gain a clearer picture of their overall business activity.

What Are Royalties?

Royalties are payments that may be earned when music is used, performed, distributed, streamed, licensed, or otherwise generates revenue.

Depending on a musician's role in creating or owning the music, royalties can come from several different sources.

For many artists, royalties represent recurring income that continues after a song, album, or project has been completed.

Why Income Tracking Matters

Musicians often have multiple revenue streams operating at the same time.

Examples may include:

  • Live performances
  • Teaching income
  • Session work
  • Merchandise sales
  • Streaming revenue
  • Royalty payments
  • Licensing income
  • Content creation revenue

Maintaining separate records for each category can make financial reviews much easier.

Performance Royalties

Performance royalties are commonly associated with music being publicly performed.

Examples may include:

  • Radio broadcasts
  • Live venues
  • Streaming services
  • Television broadcasts
  • Public performances

Musicians who receive performance royalties should maintain records of statements and payment activity throughout the year.

Mechanical Royalties

Mechanical royalties are commonly associated with the reproduction and distribution of music.

Examples may include:

  • Physical music sales
  • Digital downloads
  • Certain streaming activities

Artists and songwriters may encounter these royalty types as their music is distributed through various channels.

Streaming Revenue

Streaming income has become an increasingly important revenue source for many artists.

Platforms may include:

  • Spotify
  • Apple Music
  • Amazon Music
  • YouTube Music
  • Other streaming services

Because streaming payments are often received regularly over time, maintaining organized statements and payment records can be especially helpful.

Licensing Income

Music licensing can provide additional income opportunities beyond performances and streaming.

Examples of licensed uses may include:

  • Television productions
  • Movies
  • Commercials
  • Video games
  • Podcasts
  • Online media projects

Contracts, licensing agreements, and payment records should be organized and retained as part of a complete recordkeeping system.

Merchandise Sales Are Music Income Too

Many musicians focus exclusively on royalties while overlooking the importance of merchandise revenue.

For some artists, merchandise can become one of the largest sources of business income.

Examples may include:

  • T-shirts
  • CDs
  • Vinyl records
  • Posters
  • Stickers
  • Branded accessories

Maintaining records of merchandise sales, inventory purchases, and event sales can help provide a more complete picture of overall music-related income.

Save Royalty Statements

Royalty statements often provide valuable information regarding music income received throughout the year.

Consider organizing:

  • Royalty statements
  • Payment confirmations
  • Distribution reports
  • Streaming summaries
  • Licensing documentation

Maintaining these records can make future reviews much easier.

Create Separate Income Categories

Many musicians benefit from tracking revenue by category.

Examples may include:

  • Performance Income
  • Teaching Income
  • Session Work
  • Merchandise Sales
  • Streaming Revenue
  • Royalties
  • Licensing Income
  • Content Creation Revenue

Separate categories can provide better visibility into what parts of the business are generating income.

Common Mistakes

Combining All Income Into One Category

Different revenue streams can provide valuable information about business performance when tracked separately.

Ignoring Merchandise Revenue

Merchandise often represents a meaningful source of income and should be reviewed alongside other music-related revenue.

Not Saving Royalty Statements

Statements can help musicians monitor income activity and maintain organized records.

Waiting Until Tax Season

Income tracking is generally easier when performed throughout the year rather than all at once.

The Bottom Line

Modern musicians often earn income from many different sources. Live performances, teaching, session work, merchandise sales, streaming revenue, royalties, licensing agreements, and content creation can all contribute to the financial success of a music business.

Understanding where income comes from—and tracking each source separately— can help musicians stay organized, better understand their business, and prepare for the future with greater confidence.
